If we knew what it was we were doing,
it wouldn’t be called research, would it?

Forget about your topic and title.
It was just a way for us to meet.

  • We will not tell you what to do.
    • We might tell you what not to do.
  • Your first task is to find out
    what it is that you will study.
  • Read literature relevant to your topic,
    find an interesting topic to work on.
    • We’ll point you in the right direction.

We suggest this research process for
computer science engineering.

  1. Study related work
  2. Define research questions and hypotheses
  3. Build software to test your hypotheses
  4. Evaluate your software by running experiments
  5. Draw conclusions, and repeat if necessary

Studying the state of the art tells you
what exists and what the challenges are.

  • Understand the important technologies.
  • Find current contributions.
    • Take note of how they are evaluated.
  • Identify the gaps and open problems.

Perhaps the most challenging in research
is to come up with the right questions.

  • Design a question that captures your goal.
  • Find falsifiable hypotheses you aim to test.
  • Research questions can evolve.
    • Break them down into subquestions.
    • Specialize when you find a more pressing problem.

Build software that you and others
can use to test hypotheses.

  • Build research software, not production software.
    • Focus on correctness and flexibility.
    • Design modular, interchangeable components.
  • Solve the most difficult problems first.
    Avoid spending time on trivial issues.
    • Fail fast and often.
  • If you know it’s going to work, it’s not research.

Design an evaluation of your software
that brings evidence for your hypotheses.

  • Know before you build what you will measure.
    • Optimize toward a certain goal or target.
  • Design repeatable experiments early.
    • Re-run them often to measure improvement.
  • All experiments should be reproducible.
    • Save your code, setup, and data.

Draw conclusions based on your data.
Generalize, but document limitations.

  • Can you accept or reject your hypothesis?
  • What does this say about your context?
    • internal validity
  • What does this say about other contexts?
    • external validity
  • Be specific about what your solution addresses.
    • Report—don’t advertise. Remain objective.
